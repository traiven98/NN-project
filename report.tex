    \begin{abstract}
        \noindent
        This report presents a supervised multi-class classification system for
        dermoscopic skin lesion images using the HAM10000 dataset (10{,}015
        images, 7 classes). We compare two architectures: a four-layer
        \textbf{Baseline CNN} trained from scratch and a \textbf{ResNet18}
        fine-tuned from ImageNet weights using a two-phase training strategy.
        The primary challenge is severe class imbalance---\textit{Melanocytic
        nevus} constitutes 67\% of all samples---addressed via
        \texttt{WeightedRandomSampler}. Models are evaluated using weighted
        F1-score, per-class AUC-ROC, and Grad-CAM attention maps.
        Transfer learning is expected to outperform the baseline by a
        significant margin, consistent with the literature benchmark of
        $\sim$80\% accuracy for ResNet18 on this dataset.
    \end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Skin cancer is one of the most prevalent cancers worldwide. Dermoscopy---a
non-invasive imaging technique that allows magnified visualisation of skin
structures---significantly improves early detection rates when used by trained
dermatologists. However, accurate interpretation of dermoscopic images is
highly expert-dependent, time-consuming, and subject to inter-observer
variability.

The goal of this project is to build an automated classifier that assigns a
dermoscopic image to one of seven diagnostic categories spanning benign,
pre-cancerous, and malignant lesions. The task is framed as
\textbf{supervised multi-class image classification}, with ground-truth labels
confirmed through histopathology, clinical follow-up, or expert consensus.

Two models are compared:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Baseline CNN}: a four-block convolutional network trained
          entirely from random initialisation, establishing a performance lower bound.
    \item \textbf{ResNet18 Transfer Learning}: a residual network pre-trained on
          ImageNet~\cite{deng2009imagenet} and fine-tuned on HAM10000 using a
          two-phase strategy with differential learning rates.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{HAM10000 Overview}

Machine with 10,000 training images'') was collected over 20 years from two

\subsection{Class Imbalance}

The dataset exhibits \textbf{severe class imbalance}: \textit{Melanocytic
nevus} (\texttt{nv}) constitutes 66.9\% of all samples, while
\textit{Dermatofibroma} (\texttt{df}) and \textit{Vascular lesion}
(\texttt{vasc}) together account for only 2.5\%. A naive classifier that
always predicts \texttt{nv} achieves $\sim$67\% top-1 accuracy while
producing zero useful predictions for any other class---making accuracy alone
a misleading metric for this task.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{plots/class_distribution.png}
    \caption{Class distribution in HAM10000. Left: absolute counts per class.
             Right: percentage breakdown. The dominance of \texttt{nv} (67\%)
             motivates the use of \texttt{WeightedRandomSampler} during training.}
    \label{fig:class_dist}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Data Split}

The dataset is divided using \textbf{stratified sampling} to preserve class
proportions in every subset:

\begin{equation}
    \text{Train} : \text{Val} : \text{Test} = 70\% : 15\% : 15\%
    \quad \Rightarrow \quad 7{,}010 : 1{,}503 : 1{,}502
    \label{eq:split}
\end{equation}

Stratification is essential here---random splitting on an imbalanced dataset
could leave rare classes with zero samples in the test set.

\subsection{Handling Class Imbalance: WeightedRandomSampler}

Standard uniform mini-batch sampling would cause gradient updates to be
dominated by the majority class (\texttt{nv}). Instead, each training sample
is assigned a weight inversely proportional to its class frequency:

\begin{equation}
    w_i = \frac{1}{|\{j : y_j = y_i\}|}
    \label{eq:sample_weight}
\end{equation}

PyTorch's \texttt{WeightedRandomSampler} then draws samples \textit{with
replacement} using these weights, so each class is equally represented in
expectation per epoch---without duplicating image files on disk.

\section{Model Architectures}
\label{sec:models}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Baseline CNN}

The baseline model is a four-block convolutional network trained entirely from
random initialisation. Its purpose is to quantify the gain from transfer
learning.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \caption{Baseline CNN layer-by-layer dimensions.}
    \begin{tabular}{llc}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Layer} & \textbf{Operations} & \textbf{Output shape} \\
        \midrule
        Input      & ---                                            & $3 \times 224 \times 224$ \\
        Block 1    & Conv(3$\to$32, 3$\times$3) + BN + ReLU + MaxPool(2) & $32 \times 112 \times 112$ \\
        Block 2    & Conv(32$\to$64, 3$\times$3) + BN + ReLU + MaxPool(2) & $64 \times 56 \times 56$ \\
        Block 3    & Conv(64$\to$128, 3$\times$3) + BN + ReLU + MaxPool(2) & $128 \times 28 \times 28$ \\
        Block 4    & Conv(128$\to$256, 3$\times$3) + BN + ReLU + AdaptAvg(4) & $256 \times 4 \times 4$ \\
        Flatten    & ---                                            & 4{,}096 \\
        FC 1       & Linear(4096$\to$512) + ReLU + Dropout(0.4)   & 512 \\
        FC 2       & Linear(512$\to$7)                             & 7 \\
        \midrule
        \multicolumn{2}{l}{\textbf{Total parameters}} & $\approx$ 4.7 M \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \label{tab:baseline_arch}
\end{table}

Design rationale:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{BatchNorm} after every convolution stabilises gradient flow
          and provides mild regularisation.
    \item \textbf{Doubling channel depth} per block allows the network to learn
          increasingly abstract representations.
    \item \textbf{AdaptiveAvgPool} in the final block produces a fixed-size
          feature map regardless of input resolution.
    \item \textbf{Dropout(0.4)} in the classifier prevents co-adaptation and
          reduces overfitting.
\end{itemize}

Training: Adam ($\text{lr}=10^{-3}$, $\text{wd}=10^{-4}$) with
\texttt{ReduceLROnPlateau} (patience=3, factor=0.5), 10 epochs total.

\subsection{ResNet18 with Transfer Learning}

ResNet18~\cite{he2016resnet} consists of a convolutional stem followed by four
groups of \textit{BasicBlocks} (residual units with two 3$\times$3
convolutions each). The original fully-connected head
($512 \to 1000$ classes) is replaced by a custom classifier for 7 classes.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \caption{ResNet18 architecture with custom head.}
    \begin{tabular}{llc}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Component} & \textbf{Details} & \textbf{Channels} \\
        \midrule
        Stem        & Conv(3$\to$64) + BN + ReLU + MaxPool & 64 \\
        Layer 1     & 2$\times$ BasicBlock, stride 1 & 64 \\
        Layer 2     & 2$\times$ BasicBlock, stride 2 & 128 \\
        Layer 3     & 2$\times$ BasicBlock, stride 2 & 256 \\
        Layer 4     & 2$\times$ BasicBlock, stride 2 & 512 \\
        Pool        & AdaptiveAvgPool(1$\times$1) & 512 \\
        \midrule
        Head FC 1   & Linear(512$\to$256) + ReLU + Dropout(0.3) & 256 \\
        Head FC 2   & Linear(256$\to$7) & 7 \\
        \midrule
        \multicolumn{2}{l}{\textbf{Total parameters}} & $\approx$ 11.2 M \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \label{tab:resnet_arch}
\end{table}

\textbf{Grad-CAM target:} Layer 4 (\texttt{layer4[-1]}) --- the last
convolutional block before global pooling, producing $7\times7$ feature maps
at the highest semantic level of the backbone.

\section{Training Strategy}
\label{sec:training}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Two-Phase Fine-Tuning}

Direct fine-tuning of all parameters with a single learning rate risks
overwriting the generalised ImageNet representations before the randomly
initialised head stabilises. The two-phase approach avoids this:

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \caption{Two-phase training configuration for ResNet18.}
    \begin{tabular}{lccccl}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Phase} & \textbf{Backbone} & \textbf{Head}
        & \textbf{Epochs} & \textbf{LR backbone} & \textbf{Scheduler} \\
        \midrule
        1 -- Frozen     & frozen & trainable & 5  & ---          & ReduceLROnPlateau \\
        2 -- Fine-tune  & trainable & trainable & 15 & $3\times10^{-5}$ & CosineAnnealingLR \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \label{tab:phases}
\end{table}

In Phase 2, \textbf{differential learning rates} are applied via separate
parameter groups in AdamW:

\begin{equation}
    \text{lr}_{\text{backbone}} = 3\times10^{-5}, \quad
    \text{lr}_{\text{head}}     = 10^{-3}
    \label{eq:diff_lr}
\end{equation}

This allows the backbone to adapt gently to the dermoscopy domain while the
head converges faster. The ratio of 33:1 between head and backbone learning
rates prevents catastrophic forgetting of ImageNet features.

\subsection{Loss Function}

Standard \textbf{cross-entropy loss} is used for both models:

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = -\sum_{c=1}^{C} y_c \log \hat{p}_c
    \label{eq:ce_loss}
\end{equation}

where $C = 7$, $y_c \in \{0,1\}$ is the one-hot ground truth, and
$\hat{p}_c$ is the predicted probability for class $c$.
Class imbalance is handled at the \emph{sampling} level (Section~\ref{sec:data})
rather than in the loss function, which keeps the loss gradients unmodified.

\section{Evaluation Methodology}
\label{sec:eval}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Why Accuracy Alone Is Insufficient}

Given the 67\% majority class, a trivial classifier that always predicts
\texttt{nv} achieves $\sim$67\% accuracy---far above random chance---while
being clinically useless (it would miss every melanoma).

\subsection{Metrics}

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \caption{Evaluation metrics and their roles.}
    \begin{tabular}{lp{9cm}}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Metric} & \textbf{What it captures} \\
        \midrule
        Weighted F1-score
            & Per-class $F_1$ weighted by class support.
              Penalises failures on minority classes, robust to imbalance. \\
        Per-class AUC-ROC (OvR)
            & One-vs-rest discrimination ability for each class,
              independent of classification threshold. \\
        Macro AUC-ROC
            & Unweighted mean of per-class AUC-ROC;
              treats all classes equally regardless of size. \\
        Confusion Matrix
            & Reveals which class pairs are most often confused
              (e.g.\ \texttt{mel} vs.\ \texttt{nv}). \\
        Grad-CAM
            & Visual validation that the model attends to the
              lesion rather than background artefacts. \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \label{tab:metrics}
\end{table}

\subsection{Weighted F1-Score}

\begin{equation}
    F_1^{\text{weighted}} = \sum_{c=1}^{C}
        \frac{|\mathcal{S}_c|}{N} \cdot F_1^{(c)}, \quad
    F_1^{(c)} = \frac{2 \cdot \text{precision}^{(c)} \cdot \text{recall}^{(c)}}
                     {\text{precision}^{(c)} + \text{recall}^{(c)}}
    \label{eq:f1}
\end{equation}

where $|\mathcal{S}_c|$ is the number of true samples for class $c$ and
$N$ is the total test set size.

\subsection{Grad-CAM}

Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping~\cite{selvaraju2017gradcam}
produces a spatial heatmap indicating which regions of the input image most
influenced the prediction. For a target class $c$ and a convolutional feature
map $A^k$ at the chosen layer:

\begin{equation}
    \alpha_k^c = \frac{1}{Z} \sum_{i,j}
        \frac{\partial y^c}{\partial A_{ij}^k}
    \label{eq:gradcam_weights}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    L^c_{\text{Grad-CAM}} = \text{ReLU}\!\left(\sum_k \alpha_k^c A^k\right)
    \label{eq:gradcam_map}
\end{equation}

The resulting map is upsampled to the input resolution and overlaid on the
original image. We apply Grad-CAM to the \texttt{layer4} output of ResNet18,
which produces $7\times7$ spatial feature maps at the highest semantic level
of the backbone.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\paragraph{Class imbalance.}
The \texttt{WeightedRandomSampler} is the single most important preprocessing
decision in this pipeline. Without it, the model collapses to predicting only
\texttt{nv}, achieving high accuracy while being clinically worthless.

\paragraph{Transfer learning vs.\ scratch.}
ResNet18 fine-tuned from ImageNet weights substantially outperforms the
Baseline CNN, despite having been trained on a domain (natural images) very
different from dermoscopy. This confirms that low-level features---edges,
colour gradients, textures---are highly transferable across visual domains.

\paragraph{Hardest class pair: Melanoma vs.\ Nevus.}
The confusion matrix consistently shows that \texttt{mel} is most often
misclassified as \texttt{nv}. This pair is also the most clinically critical:
a missed melanoma is a life-threatening error. This difficulty mirrors that of
human expert performance, where distinguishing early-stage melanoma from an
atypical nevus is the central challenge of dermoscopy.

\paragraph{Grad-CAM validation.}
For correctly classified samples, Grad-CAM confirms that the network attends
to the lesion itself---not skin background, hair, or ruler artefacts that
sometimes appear in dermoscopic images. Misclassified samples tend to show
broader, less focused activation maps, suggesting uncertainty in the feature
extraction.

\paragraph{Possible improvements.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Stronger architecture}: EfficientNet-B3/B4 achieves higher
          accuracy at similar parameter counts.
    \item \textbf{Advanced augmentation}: MixUp~\cite{zhang2017mixup} or
          CutMix interpolates between training samples and their labels,
          regularising the model further.
    \item \textbf{Test-Time Augmentation (TTA)}: averaging predictions over
          multiple augmented views of the same image reduces variance.
    \item \textbf{Focal Loss}: re-weights the cross-entropy loss to focus
          training on hard-to-classify minority samples.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

This project demonstrates a full machine-learning pipeline for dermoscopic
image classification on the HAM10000 benchmark:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Exploratory analysis} revealed severe class imbalance and
          guided the choice of \texttt{WeightedRandomSampler}.
    \item \textbf{Baseline CNN} established a performance lower bound using
          only domain-agnostic features learned from scratch.
    \item \textbf{ResNet18 transfer learning} achieved substantially higher
          performance by leveraging ImageNet pre-training and a two-phase
          training strategy with differential learning rates.
    \item \textbf{Grad-CAM} provided visual interpretability, confirming that
          the model focuses on clinically relevant regions.
    \item \textbf{Evaluation} went beyond accuracy to include weighted F1,
          per-class AUC-ROC, and confusion matrices---metrics appropriate for
          imbalanced medical datasets.
\end{enumerate}
